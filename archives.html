Era One

Broadcast History — Heritage Radio

KAZM signed on the air in Sedona on November 1, 1974, and has been the town's hometown signal ever since. It began at the original AM site — the 780 AM tower on the valley floor — and grew over the decades into FM translation, reaching more of the Verde Valley and northern Arizona.

Along the way the site itself evolved: transmitters, antennas, and monitoring gear were upgraded and rebuilt as the technology changed, while the station kept its easy, local sound. Fifty years of broadcasting leaves a lot of stories — the engineers, the voices, the long overnight shifts, and the equipment that came and went.

Era Two

Reinvention — the broadcast-powered micro-campus

In its later years, KAZM set out to become something new. The station wasn't only selling advertising anymore — it was building toward a broadcast-powered micro-campus: a set of activities designed to support one another, financially and operationally, around a single broadcast site.

The vision tied together, on purpose:

  • Radio broadcasting
  • NOAA weather participation
  • Volunteer field operations
  • Signal Watch
  • Tower Watch
  • Parking
  • Hipcamp scheduling
  • Echo Ridge wellness
  • Yoga & meditation
  • Sound healing
  • Astronomy
  • Community education
  • Historic tower preservation

None of these were random ideas. Each was meant to help keep a small, independent, heritage station standing on its own.

Stewardship

Signal Watch

Signal Watch was never about camping. It was a volunteer stewardship program for a remote transmitter site — a way to keep eyes on a historic radio facility and the conditions its signal traveled through.

Its purpose was to:

  • Protect a remote transmitter site
  • Discourage theft and vandalism
  • Log propagation
  • Monitor weather
  • Observe RF conditions
  • Document lightning
  • Check antennas
  • Create a volunteer presence
  • Preserve a historic radio facility

The program was built out in detail: Signal Watch signage, volunteer IDs, tower sectors, observation logs, nightly patrol routes, weather logs, and signal reports — authorized volunteers only, with FCC considerations and written operating procedures. Reserved station-operations parking was for staff and approved volunteers, not general public camping.

The reservation system

Hipcamp

Hipcamp started as just another income stream. It became something more specific: the reservation system for Signal Watch. Guests weren't simply booking a campsite — they were reserving participation in the station's volunteer stewardship program.

Operations

Parking

Parking turned out to matter more than it sounds. It became part of the operational model that kept the site working: station operations, volunteer access, and a small amount of passive income to support the infrastructure.

The plan described two designated lots — roughly twenty spaces total — reserved for station staff and approved Signal Watch volunteers, with no long-term commercial storage. It was station-operations parking first, listed on a neighbor platform second.

Engineering Notebook

The experiments and field notes — the kind of quiet tests radio people run when they have a transmitter, a dark sky, and a question worth asking. Curiosity, not claims.

Engineering log

The Harmonic Tone Project

For about a year, after normal programming ended, KAZM used the 5,000-watt 780 AM transmitter for a low-frequency audio test. Four tones rotated through the overnight hours, each running for five minutes before the next began. At the end of the cycle, all four tones played together for five minutes, then the sequence started again.

The original test values were slightly detuned — 54.7, 72.3, 89.4, and 111.0 Hz — before the setup was simplified to 54, 72, 84, and 111 Hz for consistency.

The purpose was audio engineering curiosity: how simple low-frequency tones interacted, how they felt in the station monitor chain, and how stable they remained over long unattended runs. It was not presented as a medical treatment, a scientific claim, or proof of any paranormal effect.

Engineering log

The Phase-Walk Experiment

Separate from the tone cycle, KAZM also tested a phase-walk pattern. The generated waveform was advanced by a tiny 0.01 phase step, just enough that the pattern never landed in exactly the same place twice.

To the ear, the change was subtle. On a scope or visualization it was the interesting part: over time the waveform traced a slow triangular path instead of repeating as a fixed loop.

This was a waveform geometry test — not a frequency experiment, and not a change to the AM carrier. It was about watching how tiny phase movement accumulated over a long night.

Propagation

Night Skywave & Alaska Reports

After sunset, 780 AM behaves differently. The same signal that serves Sedona by day can lift into the ionosphere at night and return hundreds — sometimes thousands — of miles away.

Distant listeners and signal watchers have caught the station well beyond Arizona on favorable nights; some reports placed it as far north as Alaska. That was never normal coverage — just the sky opening a path for a local signal to travel farther than anyone on the ground expected.

Field notes

The night we chased the aurora

During an exceptionally strong geomagnetic storm, the sky above Sedona filled with rare purple and red auroral light. We watched the display, compared notes, kept an eye on the instrumentation, and experimented with transmitter conditions to see if anything unusual could be detected.

Did the transmitter influence the lights? No evidence ever suggested it did — but that wasn't the point. The point was being there, curious, when nature put on a show.

Field notes

The deer that loved the weather station

Not every antenna at KAZM was on the tower. For years the local deer rubbed their antlers on our weather station until it picked up the station's own signal and buzzed with a little interference — an accidental mini-antenna they clearly enjoyed.

When the weather readings fluttered for no obvious reason, we usually knew exactly who was visiting the site that night.

A chapter of its own

The Arizona State Land Years

This is one of the most important chapters — not because of any legal dispute, but because it's the story of trying to keep a small independent broadcaster alive.

For years the station operated on Arizona State Trust Land, and it's where the reinvention really took shape: the creative ideas developed to sustain the station, the Signal Watch and volunteer stewardship model, and a genuine effort to build something durable around a heritage site. In the end the lease came to a close — but the determination to preserve KAZM's legacy is exactly why these archives exist.
