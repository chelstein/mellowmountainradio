Engineering log

The Harmonic Tone Project

For about a year, after normal programming ended, KAZM used the 5,000-watt 780 AM transmitter for a low-frequency audio test. Four tones rotated through the overnight hours, each running for five minutes before the next began. At the end of the cycle, all four tones played together for five minutes, then the sequence started again.

The original test values were slightly detuned — 54.7, 72.3, 89.4, and 111.0 Hz — before the setup was simplified to 54, 72, 84, and 111 Hz for consistency.

The purpose was audio engineering curiosity: how simple low-frequency tones interacted, how they felt in the station monitor chain, and how stable they remained over long unattended runs. It was not presented as a medical treatment, a scientific claim, or proof of any paranormal effect — simply one of those experiments radio people try when they have a transmitter, a quiet night, and a question worth testing.

Engineering log

The Phase-Walk Experiment

Separate from the tone cycle, KAZM also tested a phase-walk pattern. In that experiment, the generated waveform was advanced by a tiny 0.01 phase step, just enough that the pattern never landed in exactly the same place twice.

To the ear, the change was subtle. On a scope or visualization, it was the interesting part: over time the waveform traced a slow triangular path instead of repeating as a fixed loop.

This was a waveform geometry test — not a frequency experiment, and not a change to the AM carrier. It was about watching how tiny phase movement accumulated over time: the kind of quiet engineering test that makes perfect sense in a radio station after midnight.

Signal Watch

Watching the conditions

KAZM has always been more than a signal leaving a tower. It has also been a station watching the conditions that signal travels through.

Signal Watch is our name for that habit: keeping an eye on the transmitter, the weather, the Moon, the ionosphere, geomagnetic activity, lightning, and the reports from listeners who catch 780 AM far from Sedona after sunset.

None of these readings control the station. But they are part of the environment every radio wave moves through. For a station in the red rocks, watching the sky is part of listening to the signal.

Local history

Tower Watch

At one point, the idea expanded beyond the website and into the land around the old radio site. The thought was simple: let a small number of self-contained campers stay near the historic tower area and experience the station from the ground up.

It was never meant to be a regular campground. It was more like a Signal Watch post — a place for people who understood that a radio tower at sunset is part machine, part landmark, and part local history.

Visitors could listen to KAZM, watch the sky change over the Verde Valley, log reception conditions, and see the red rocks from the same landscape the signal was leaving every night.

Propagation

Night Skywave & Alaska Reports

After sunset, 780 AM behaves differently. The same signal that serves Sedona by day can lift into the ionosphere at night and return hundreds — sometimes thousands — of miles away.

Over the years, KAZM has heard from distant listeners and signal watchers who caught the station far beyond Arizona during favorable nighttime conditions. Some reports placed the signal as far north as Alaska.

That does not mean Alaska was part of the normal service area. It means the sky was doing what AM radio people have watched for a century: opening a path for a local signal to travel farther than anyone on the ground expected.
