Taking a small station's case to Washington

Zero Trust Radio

Here's a chapter I never expected to be writing. The Emergency Alert System runs on aging, largely proprietary hardware, and when one vendor (Sage) started exiting the market with no real replacement plan, it looked to me like a national security problem hiding inside a procurement problem — especially for small, independent, rural stations like ours that can't just absorb whatever a single vendor decides to do. So I filed comments with the FCC about it. More than once.

  • July 18, 2025 — PS Docket No. 15-94, Filing ID 10718008106348. The small-market/independent broadcaster's stake in EAS modernization — the case that the stations with the fewest resources are the ones most exposed when the system gets fragile.
  • July 31, 2025 — PS Dockets 15-94 & 22-329, Filing ID 10731136229707, filed as Cutter Grind Broadcasting / Mellow Mountain Radio. The full package: eight documents laying out a framework I called Zero Trust Radio — software-defined EAS running on open orchestration instead of closed vendor hardware, a two-layer Message Context Protocol (MCP) so alerts can be machine-verified across systems, and an SDR-based verification net I nicknamed "The Listening Grid" to confirm, in real time, that an alert tone actually went out over the air instead of trusting a coverage map.
  • September 22, 2025 — PS Docket No. 25-224, filed with Ryan Thompson. Comments on the broader alerting-modernization proceeding — the question of whether to overhaul or retire the legacy EAS architecture entirely.

The July 31 filing came down to five concrete asks: formally recognize software-defined EAS architectures; put independent station owners and engineers — the people who actually keep this equipment running — on the regulatory panels that decide its future; investigate what Sage's exit really does to rural redundancy; support open frameworks like MCP instead of locking the whole system to one vendor's format; and stand up a real national pilot to test the architecture somewhere before mandating anything.

I won't pretend I know how much of that the Commission will act on — most of it is still working through the process. But here's the part I can point to and say, plainly, this happened: the FCC's own Report and Order on Modernizing the Nation's Alerting Systems (FCC 26-38, adopted June 25, 2026), in the paragraph explaining why the Commission chose not to overhaul or phase out the legacy EAS architecture, cites the September 22 comment by name: “Charles Helstein, Ryan Thompson, et al., Comments, PS Docket No. 25-224, at 1 (rec. Sept. 22, 2025).” A little AM station's engineer and production director, on the record, in a federal order.

That's the whole point of Signal Watch applied to a bigger map: presence is preservation. Somebody has to actually show up — to the tower at 2 a.m., or to an FCC docket — or the system quietly gets worse and nobody who could've said something did.

— Chuck
